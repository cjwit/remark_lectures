Vaudeville * Theatrical shows derived from minstrelsy * By 1900, this is the most important mechanism for popularizing sheet music sales (from Tin Pan Alley) * Series of performances with no story (variety show) .image-100[![Vaudeville]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/vaudeville.png)] --- # Early Industry .image-float-right[![Recording]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/recording.png)] * Context in the United States * Migration from the South to the North, country to city * Roaring 20s into the Great Depression * Prohibition (18th Amendment) * Reacting to World War 1 * Social unrest * Racism * The Ku Klux Klan * Jim Crow laws (1890 to 1965) --- # Early Industry * The music industry * National popular culture via radio, film, phonograph * Vaudeville’s popularity wanes, replaced by Broadway * Radio * Commercial stations introduced in 1920 * “Democratizes” music, inexpensive * In Hollywood * Films synchronized with sound, talkies, star singers * Los Angeles starts to compete with New York as the center of the industry by the end of the 30s --- # The Post-War Era (1946-54) * Larger companies focus on mainstream pop * Return of independent labels * Rise of the big singers * By 1946, focus moves away from instrumentalists, bandleaders .image-100[![Vaudeville]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/nostalgia.png)] --- class: middle, center ### Bing Crosby: "Where the Blue of the Night" --- class: middle, center ### Louis Jordan: “Choo Choo Ch’Boogie” --- # Advent of Rock and Roll * Came with major musical changes * Marginal styles dominate the mainstream * R&B and country are no longer marketed to a specific population * Teenage markets (Baby boomers) * Shared characteristics and a strong group identity * Unlike previous generations, the economic rise permitted allowances—jobs for teens—leading to spending money * Many hits were romantic songs, crooners for the young generation * New levels of racial tension following 1954s Brown vs. Board of Education (Supreme Court ended racial segregation in public schools) --- class: middle, center ### Chuck Berry: “Maybellene” --- class: middle, center ### Beach Boys: “Surfin USA” (1963) --- class: middle, center ### Little Richard: “Tutti Frutti” --- # Cover Versions * Copies of previously recorded performances * Adaptations of the original, cashing in on successes * Often white performances of R&B tunes * Further moved rock and roll into white markets * The “Little Bird Told Me” decision * 1948: R&B star Paula Watson’s “A Little Bird Told Me” (on Supreme Records) was covered by Decca’s Evelyn Knight * Supreme sued Decca and lost - the judge ruled that musical arrangements could not be copyrighted * Opened the door to the long history of cover songs --- class: middle, center ### Pat Boone: “Tutti Fruitti” --- class: middle, center ### Elvis Presley: “Tutti Fruitti” --- # Music and Civil Rights * Civil Rights as a National Issue * Rosa Parks in 1955, for example * Shifting tastes in popular music markets * Early rock based on southern R&B styles * Chuck Berry (“good music for white people”), Ray Charles, and others * Lyrics did not portray activist themes directly * No outright demand for rights * Girl groups in the 60s redefine the pop sound * Shirelles, Ronettes * Phil Spector, the Brill Building * Aesthetic change alongside sit ins, March on Washington... --- # Barry Gordy and Motown .image-float-right[![Barry Gordy]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/gordy.png)] * 1962: 42% of singles are by black artists * Highlights the change in popular tastes * Motown Records, owned by Berry Gordy * Most significant black owned record label ever * Based in Detroit, MI * Incorporate white tastes while maintaining a “black sound” * Upbeat, danceable, smooth, “slick” studio aesthetic * Not threatening in any way (in tone or lyrical content) --- class: middle, center ### The Marvelettes: “Please Mr. Postman” --- class: middle, center ### The Temptations: “My Girl” (1965) --- # Sam Cooke * “A Change is Gonna Come” (1965) .image-float-right[![Sam Cooke]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/sam-cooke.png)] * Inspired by Dylan’s “Blowing in the Wind” * Shot to death before the single was released * Two weeks later, Malcom X assassinated * The song became an anthem of the civil rights movement --- # The 1970s .image-30.image-float-right[![Sam Cooke]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/1970s.png)] * Change in economic, political activity * Most Americans have grown weary of Vietnam, Civil Rights Movement * The Oil Crisis in 1973 * Economic inflation hits hard * Cynicism about politics * Watergate hearings, Nixon’s resignation in 1974 * Popular music marketing * Increasingly focused on two categories: a new generation of teens (b. late 1950s, early 60s) and adults 25-40 (b. after WWII) * Heavy focus on “subgenres,” targeted advertising --- # The Business * Corporate consolidation * 6 companies responsible for over 80% of sales * Columbia/CBS, Warner Communications, RCA Victor, Capitol-EMI, MCA, United Artists-MGM * The industry got riskier during the ‘70s * Depended on fewer LPs to make a profit * Stars negotiated multimillion dollar contracts * Continued with crash in 1979, into the ‘80s --- # The 1980s * Music Television (and image) * Recovery from superstars who could capture MTV’s audiences * Decline of lucrative disco * “Pirating” (coincides with the cassette) * By ’84, cassettes surpass vinyl sales * Crash of the 1980s * National recession, deregulation * Competition from new forms of entertainment --- # And What Came Next? * Superstars and Supergroups * Brittany Spears, Christina Aguilera * New Kids, *NSync, Backstreet, etc… * All built on histories of their own * Change in how music is made * Technology allows for collaboration * Increasing importance of production (both musical and otherwise) * Expanding markets (and tech) --- class: middle, center ### New Edition: “Candy Girl” (1983) --- class: middle, center ### New Kids on the Block: “Step By Step” (1990) --- class: middle, center ### Boyz II Men: “On Bended Knee” (1994) --- class: middle, center ### *NSync: “I Want You Back” (1998) --- class: middle, center ### Madonna: “Like a Prayer” (1989) --- class: middle, center ### Britney Spears: “… Baby One More Time” (1999) --- class: middle, center ### Christina Aguilera: "Genie in a Bottle” (1999) --- class: middle, center --- class: title, center, middle # Roots of the American Music Industry ![BGSU]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/bgsu.png) ### Christopher Witulski, Ph.D. ### College of Musical Arts
