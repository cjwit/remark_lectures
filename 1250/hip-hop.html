Recorded some early tracks --- class: middle, center ### Afrika Bambaataa and Soulsonic Force: “Planet Rock” --- # Rap * DJs used mics for announcements * Kool Herc was one of the first to recite rhymes * Based on “toasting”: poetic storytelling based in West African traditions (“dozens”) * All about keeping the crowd hype for parties * DJs begin to hire MCs * Their rhymes, chants get longer * End up becoming the focal point --- class: middle, center ### Clip from *Beat Street* (1984) --- # Commercial Success * “Rapper’s Delight” * Set up precedents for later hip hop * Popularized the term “rapper” for MC * Established Sugar Hill Records in early 80s rap * Black owned indie label based in NJ * Based on Chic’s “Good Times” * Replayed in the studio by session musicians * Three rappers, alternating and intertwining lyrics --- class: middle, center ### Sugarhill Gang: “Rapper’s Delight” --- # Commercial Success * “The Message” * Slow tempo, social realism * Grim, cinematic portrait of South Bronx life * Helped establish the idea of street credibility, realness that are valued by rap musicians and audiences today * Other early hits * Kurtis Blow: “The Breaks” (#4 R&B, #87 pop) * “Planet Rock” (#4 R&B, #48 pop) --- class: middle, center ### Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five: “The Message” --- # Long Island * Moving to the ‘burbs * Def Jam Records * Russell Simmons and Rick Rubin * Moving past soul/R&B and punk scenes * Impressive results * Brought Run-DMC to white audiences * Brought the Beastie Boys to black audiences * Cultivating a street image (Run-DMC) and crossover potential --- class: middle, center ### Run-DMC: “Walk This Way” --- class: middle, center ### Beastie Boys: “Fight for Your Right” --- # Meanwhile, in LA... * Watts * Almost a week of riots in response to police brutality in 1965, $40m in damage, 34 dead .image-100[![LA Map]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/la-map.png)] --- # Meanwhile, in LA... * Watts (right after Civil Rights Act) * Almost a week of riots in response to police brutality in 1965, $40m in damage, 34+ dead * Aftermath * Street gangs organize with Black Power movements like the Black Panthers * FBI and LAPD respond with aggression, incarceration, and assassination of leadership --- # Meanwhile, in LA... * Recession leads to fewer jobs * Major factories (like GM) leave the region * 70,000 laid off workers by 1982 * Black Power movements wane * Gangs reorganize: Avenue Cribs (Crips) rise * Others organize as Bloods in opposition * Dramatic increase in gang violence * 1972: 29 gang-related murders * 1978: 70 gang-related murders --- # Meanwhile, in LA... * Huge influx of drugs from Contras * Relation to Reagan’s policies in Nicaragua * Huge money in controlling neighborhoods * Major shift in gang violence * Money, guns on the streets * Both dealers and addicts contribute to a fast, dramatic change in neighborhoods * Gangs gain influence across urban and suburban areas nationwide --- # Meanwhile, in LA... * Taste focused on dance, techno * Fashion, beats grow out of these scenes * All about the party, until drugs change the streets and rappers shift toward reality .image-float-right[![Ice-T]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/ice-t.png)] * Ice-T’s “6’N the Mornin’” * 1986 B-Side * Based on Schoolly D’s “P.S.K.” * Sang a day in the life of a Crip * Changed what you can rap --- # N.W.A. * From Compton, also 1986 * Controversial group due to lyrics, violence * Included Easy-E, Dr. Dre, Ice Cube * Members from the techno-heavy scene * “Boyz-n-the-Hood” * Written by Ice Cube, performed by Easy-E * Detailed life in a gang, from a gang member --- class: middle, center ### N.W.A.: “Straight Outta Compton” --- # Rodney King * Video from Smithsonian ([link](www.smithsonianchannel.com/shows/the-lost-tapes/la-riots/1004513/3437439)) * Relationship with Korean population * War on Youth * Criminalization of youth (not just gang) activities * Increase in incarceration rates * Clinton’s three strikes * Drop in homicide rates, criticism for wide ranging wrongful arrests --- #
